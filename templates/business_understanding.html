Section 01

System Objective

Animal abandonment is one of the most critical welfare problems in Colombia. Official datasets confirming abandonment events do not exist — making direct prediction impossible. PetWatch bridges this gap by building a risk proxy signal from institutional open data.

The system's goal is to detect patterns and estimate the abandonment risk level in different zones of Bogotá and Colombia, using open civic data and Machine Learning. It analyzes variables related to animal abuse, veterinary emergencies, institutional coverage, territorial recurrence, and animal vulnerability — producing a composite risk score per locality.

Within scope

Risk pattern detection by locality and zone
Prediction of abandonment risk level — HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW
Geographic cluster analysis of vulnerable zones
Variable importance ranking for abandonment drivers
Model comparison across RF, Logistic Regression, K-Means

Out of scope

Real-time abandonment detection
Confirming specific animals were abandoned
Proving causal relationships — correlation is not causation
Coverage outside Bogotá and Cali in the initial phase
Individual-level risk prediction per animal

Section 02

Key Questions the Model Must Answer

These questions define the analytical direction of PetWatch. Select each to expand the rationale behind it.

Q1 Which localities present the highest risk of animal abandonment? →
This is the primary output. By combining abuse rates, emergency counts, and institutional coverage per locality, the model produces a risk ranking across Bogotá's 20 localities — allowing authorities to prioritize resource deployment where the signal is strongest.
Q2 Are there geographic patterns associated with abandonment? →
K-Means clustering will group localities by their welfare indicator profiles. The hypothesis is that peripheral urban zones with low institutional presence form natural clusters of high vulnerability — validating spatially-targeted policy interventions.
Q3 Which variables influence abandonment risk the most? →
Random Forest's feature importance will rank the contribution of each variable — abuse cases, emergency volume, microchip coverage, castration rates — providing interpretable evidence for which institutional factors matter most and guiding future data collection.
Q4 Can temporal or recurring patterns be identified? →
Date fields in the dataset allow exploration of monthly and seasonal patterns. Spikes in abuse or emergency cases at specific periods may correlate with known social dynamics — enabling predictive calendar-based alerts for prevention campaigns.
Q5 Which algorithm performs best for abandonment risk detection? →
The three candidates — Random Forest, Logistic Regression, and K-Means — each address a different analytical dimension: classification, probability estimation, and unsupervised clustering. Phase 4 compares them using Accuracy, F1-Score, AUC-ROC, and interpretability criteria.

Working Hypotheses

H1

Higher abuse rates correlate with higher abandonment risk

Localities with elevated abuse reports signal irresponsible ownership and social tolerance for animal mistreatment — both direct precursors to abandonment.

H2

Lower microchip coverage indicates less responsible ownership

Microchip registration is a proxy for owner commitment. Localities with low identification rates are likely to have more unregistered, unsupervised animals — increasing vulnerability.

H3

High veterinary emergency volume signals greater animal vulnerability

A high volume of veterinary emergencies suggests animals in poor condition — possibly already neglected — and indicates stress on the local welfare infrastructure.

H4

Low institutional presence increases territorial risk

Localities with few brigades, low castration rates, and scarce citizen reports may reflect institutional absence — leaving animals without support and owners without access to responsible ownership programs.

H5

Repeating territorial patterns exist across periods

High-risk localities in one period are likely to show risk again — suggesting structural socioeconomic factors rather than random events. This justifies a monitoring and retraining cycle.

H6

Urban density amplifies visibility of animal welfare problems

Dense localities generate more reports — not because the problem is worse, but because more citizens observe and report it. This reporting bias must be accounted for during evaluation.

Section 05

Identified Limitations & Risks

CRISP-ML(Q) requires explicit risk identification before modeling. These limitations are acknowledged and incorporated into the project's design decisions.

01

No Direct Abandonment Data

No official Colombian dataset records confirmed abandonment events. All predictions are risk estimations based on indirect proxy indicators — not confirmed facts.

02

Data Quality Variability

Open datasets contain missing values, inconsistent entries, and incomplete records. The cleaning pipeline in Phase 2 addresses these, but residual noise may affect model performance.

03

Geographic Bias

Data is concentrated in urban Bogotá and Cali. Rural zones and smaller municipalities are underrepresented — limiting generalization across all of Colombia.

04

Correlation ≠ Causation

The model identifies statistical patterns and associations. It does not establish causal mechanisms. High abuse in a locality does not mean every animal there will be abandoned.

05

Dataset Integration Artifacts

Merging 5 datasets from different sources introduces structural differences in scale, granularity, and temporal coverage. Some columns required imputation that may introduce bias.

06

Risk Interpretation Boundaries

The system outputs a risk score — not a determination of abandonment. Outputs must be interpreted as decision-support signals, not conclusive evidence, by authorities.

Section 06

Target Stakeholders

PetWatch is designed for two primary user groups with distinct but complementary needs.

Primary

Animal Protection Entities & Local Authorities

Need to identify high-risk localities to deploy veterinary brigades, inspection teams, and adoption campaigns. PetWatch provides a data-driven prioritization tool for resource allocation.

Secondary

Citizens & Animal Welfare Organizations

Can use the risk map to understand which zones require community attention and to reinforce citizen reporting behavior — feeding back into the data pipeline over time.

Academic

Universidad de Cundinamarca — ML Research

Demonstrates the full CRISP-ML pipeline applied to a real Colombian social problem — from open data integration to model deployment and monitoring.
